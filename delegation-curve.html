The Delegation Curve

I've noticed something about how my relationship with AI has changed. Not the tools — those change every five minutes — but the mental model. What I think it is, what I think I owe it, how much I trust it. I've gone through at least five distinct versions of that relationship, and I'm slightly embarrassed by some of the earlier ones.

🦠

Stage 1 — The Oracle

"It knows things. I shall ask it things."

I used it like a very good search engine. A better Stack Overflow. I'd ask it a specific question, evaluate the answer with appropriate scepticism, and get on with things. The AI had no more agency than a textbook. I was very much in charge. Very professional. Very much missing the point entirely.

🐟

Stage 2 — The Co-Pilot

"I drive. You can take the wheel if I say so."

I started letting it write code. I'd describe a problem, it would draft something, I'd iterate. It was like pair programming with someone who never gets tired, never gets defensive, and never needs a coffee break. I was still very much in the driving seat — emphasising this to myself more than was probably necessary — but the collaboration was real and the pace picked up considerably.

🦕

Stage 3 — The Micromanager

"Small tasks only. I'm watching."

I'd seen it confidently produce wrong things, so I adapted the way anyone adapts when they've been surprised by a new colleague: I gave it very small tasks, reviewed every line with visible suspicion, tested manually after each step, and added new scope only once I'd personally confirmed the last bit hadn't quietly broken everything. I told myself this was professional rigour. It was also, in hindsight, exhausting and slightly paranoid. I had become the bottleneck.

🧍

Stage 4 — The Vibe Coder

"Just build the thing. I'll look if it explodes."

Eventually I got lazy — or, more charitably, gained confidence. I stopped reading every line and started checking outcomes. The velocity was genuinely extraordinary. Features appeared. Systems materialised. I felt very productive right up until the point where something broke in a way I couldn't diagnose, because I'd lost track of what was actually in the codebase several hundred lines of AI output ago. My conclusion at the time: the tool couldn't handle serious work. My conclusion now: the tool was fine. I had just stopped thinking.

🧑‍💼

Stage 5 — The Manager

"Here's the spec. Here are the interfaces. Tell me when it's done."

This is where I am now, and it required accepting that the problem was never the AI — it was me treating it like a magic box rather than something I needed to actually manage. I write a spec first. I decompose the work into chunks small enough to fit in context without losing coherence. I define the interfaces between components before anything gets built. I review the seams rather than the implementation. It feels less like coding and more like being a technical lead, which is either a promotion or a warning sign depending on how you look at it.

🚀 Stage 6 — I genuinely don't know

Each stage has handed over more responsibility than felt comfortable at the time. I don't see why that stops. My best guesses, in roughly ascending order of how much they unsettle me:

🏗

Managing a team of agents

Concurrent agents on independent components, each with a spec and a test suite. I set the architecture, resolve conflicts, verify integration. Like running an actual team, except everyone responds instantly and nobody asks for a raise.

🎯

Describing outcomes, not systems

I stop thinking about components at all. I describe what I need the thing to do, for whom, under what constraints — and the how becomes someone else's problem. Which is either liberation or abdication, I'm not sure.

📡

Evaluating, not building

The contribution shifts entirely to taste and judgment. I choose between AI-generated solutions rather than directing them. Less engineering, more editorial. I'm not sure I'm ready to admit that might be fine.

🌱

Starting over as a beginner

Every abstraction layer in computing history has made the previous layer's expertise less relevant. This one might be big enough to reset the whole stack. I'd be learning something new, from scratch, which is either exciting or terrifying depending on the day.

The honest bit

Looking back, every transition felt like giving up control — and every transition was also when things got significantly more interesting. I don't think I'm describing a universal law here. This is just what happened to me, in roughly this order, over a fairly short span of time. I suspect I'm not the only one who went through the paranoid micromanagement phase and called it professionalism. But I'm genuinely curious where it goes from here — and slightly nervous that by the time I figure it out, the answer will have changed again.
